The representation covers all types of trusts and estates, including probate, from the simplest to the most sophisticated. Simple estates may only require marshaling assets, paying creditors, and distributing assets to beneficiaries.

Sophisticated estates may require the transfer or sale of businesses and real estate for the benefit of the next generation, tax planning, preparation of tax returns, and representation before the Internal Revenue Service and other taxing authorities on audits and related matters.

We also institute post-mortem estate planning that can further minimize or even eliminate income taxes, federal estate taxes, or New York estate taxes.

### Probate When There Is a Will

Many people are confused about how a Will is probated. Probate refers to the legal process of administering an estate. The law mandates court supervision before property passes to heirs and beneficiaries. The probate process typically includes these steps:

- The Will is submitted to the appropriate probate court
- The court appoints the executor
- Estate assets are gathered
- Notice is given to heirs and creditors
- Debts of the estate are paid
- An accounting is filed with the probate court
- Money and assets are distributed according to the Will

As executor or administrator, you may be faced with challenges such as real estate or businesses that must be sold, pending legal claims against the estate, tax issues, and dissatisfied beneficiaries who threaten litigation. A knowledgeable probate attorney can help you navigate these obstacles and provide the legal guidance needed when probating a Will.

### Trust Estate Settlement Process

Settlement of a trust estate involves the process necessary to transfer asset ownership from the deceased person's trust to the people entitled to receive the assets under the provisions of the trust. If the trust was unfunded, assets may first need to be transferred from the decedent's probate estate to the trust in accordance with the decedent's Will.

### Overview of Duties of Trustee and Executor

The basic duties of the executor or trustee, working with attorneys, accountants, investment advisors, family members, and other interested parties, generally include:

- Collecting, inventorying, valuing, and conservatively managing assets
- Keeping accurate records and preparing statements and final accountings
- Coordinating post-death tax and estate planning
- Overseeing the sale of real estate and personal property
- Protecting, preserving, and investing assets
- Settling claims and paying debts
- Preparing, filing, and paying final income tax returns, estate tax returns, and estate income tax returns
- Preparing a distribution plan and transferring remaining assets to the proper parties

## Will Contests

By law, Wills are supposed to go through probate under court supervision. However, executors and administrators do not always follow the required legal steps. When that happens, beneficiaries often suffer because inheritances are not distributed the way the decedent intended.

Common scenarios include:

- Distributing property outside of probate
- Starting probate but failing to complete it
- Coercing the decedent into writing a Will that favored a particular person

## Trust Litigation

Trustees have a great deal of responsibility. If they are uncooperative, fail to communicate, or engage in misconduct, beneficiaries can be left in a difficult position. Sometimes trustees refuse to provide an accounting, make unwise investments, rewrite the trust to favor themselves, or withhold inheritances without explanation.

Trusts should be handled correctly under the law. When a trustee fails to administer a trust properly, beneficiaries may need to take legal steps to hold the trustee accountable. We assist clients in obtaining inheritances, removing trustees, executors, or administrators, and seeking reimbursement to the trust or estate for mistakes or misconduct.
