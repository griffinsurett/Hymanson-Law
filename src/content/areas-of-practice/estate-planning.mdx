The estate planning and tax strategies utilized include a myriad of tools to transfer wealth during your lifetime or after. They include wills, trusts, partnerships, corporations, and limited liability companies. For clients who want to leave money to charities, we can design and implement plans that fulfill those goals.

## Proper estate planning is necessary to have your plan carried out.

If you plan properly, your loved ones will be cared for and protected from creditors, divorce, and taxes. Below is a description of the planning documents we provide to help you take care of yourself and your family.

### Revocable Living Trust

A Revocable Living Trust distributes property much the same way a Will does. It accomplishes the distribution without probate court supervision or interference. Instead, a less expensive, less time-consuming, private process is used. The only technical difference is that title to property is in the name of the Living Trust, and you, or you and your spouse, are the trustees. Many Living Trusts also incorporate credit shelter provisions to maximize tax savings for your heirs.

Privacy is another benefit often cited by Revocable Living Trust users. When a person passes away, his or her Will becomes a public document that is easily accessible. A Revocable Living Trust may never be seen by anyone other than the trustee or trustees. According to statistics, Wills are also far more likely to be successfully challenged than Living Trusts.

Our firm strongly believes that when people have a primary goal of avoiding inheritance problems for their heirs, a Revocable Living Trust is one of the best ways to transfer assets, even when distributing a modest estate.

### Last Will and Testament

A Will is the most common document used in estate planning for giving away possessions after death. This writing instructs a probate court on how to distribute your assets after all of your bills are paid. A properly drafted Will details what items, or what percentage of the estate, heirs are to receive. It also states how taxes are handled. It can also include legally binding instructions on funeral and burial preferences, identify the people you want to administer your estate, nominate guardians for minor children, and outline age restrictions for beneficiaries.

In addition to handling the distribution of your property, a Will can also save money on estate taxes, keep money in trust for young beneficiaries, and provide guardianship nominations.

### Irrevocable Trust

An Irrevocable Trust can be used to protect your heirs from creditors, divorce, and unnecessary taxes. Irrevocable Trusts are also used to assist in special needs planning for children and grandchildren.

### Living Wills

A Living Will directs a doctor to use or not use extreme life-saving measures, such as life support, if you are in an irrecoverable coma or in a persistent vegetative state. Because the Living Will was drafted, signed, and notarized while the patient was conscious and of sound mind, doctors must follow its directives. This allows you to make important decisions ahead of time and removes the burden of those difficult decisions from loved ones.

### Health Care Power of Attorney

A Health Care Power of Attorney empowers someone else to make treatment decisions if you are incapacitated. This document covers situations where you are unconscious and a decision is needed regarding surgery or other care. Health Care Power of Attorney documents complement Living Wills by making sure your health care decisions are made by specific people you choose.

Far too often, a doctor is left waiting for the closest living family members to agree on a decision. A Health Care Power of Attorney can prevent those issues before they arise.

### Financial Power of Attorney

A Durable Power of Attorney empowers someone else to handle your financial and legal affairs while you are competent, after you become incompetent, or both. In many instances, these documents are used by married couples as a matter of convenience so that one spouse can close on a new home, change banking information, or handle other matters that require approval from both spouses.

In other cases, it is essential for an elderly parent to give this power to a child or other loved one who can step in if competency declines or if routine household affairs need to be handled. It is also important for unmarried individuals to appoint someone should they need assistance with financial affairs.

### Special Needs Estate Planning

Parents or grandparents of children or grandchildren with special needs frequently spend years addressing immediate problems one day at a time without planning far enough ahead. Special needs estate planning helps ensure that your loved ones will be cared for when you are no longer able to provide that care yourself.
